Legacy Admissions at Georgetown: Student Attitudes and the Role of Information

GOVT 2241 Class Survey Draft Memo

Lucio Bernal, Otto Dierig, Owen Khanna

April 2026

Introduction

Legacy admissions is the practice of giving preferential consideration to applicants whose family members previously attended an institution. This practice has long been one of the most contested features of elite university admissions. At Georgetown specifically, the stakes are high. 25-30% of Georgetown’s class of 2026 are legacy students, meaning Georgetown enrolled more legacies than Hispanic/Latino students (12% of the class of 2028), Black students (9% of the class of 2028), or Native American students (1% of the class of 2028) combined (Georgetown University, 2024). According to the Georgetown Hoya, children of Georgetown graduates were nearly three times more likely to be admitted than applicants without parental connections (Steele & Toscano, 2025). These numbers make Georgetown an important case study, where legacy preferences are a structural feature of how the university selects its student body. Understanding the scale and perceived legitimacy of this practice among current students is key before any reform can be considered by the university.

The question of whether Georgetown students themselves value legacy admissions has direct implications for the university’s path forward. After a two-year review of its admissions processes, Georgetown announced in 2025 that it would not make any changes to its legacy admissions practices, arguing that the pool of legacy applicants represents a similarly broad array of backgrounds as the regular applicant pool (Georgetown University, 2025). Despite this claim, legacy admissions remains a controversial topic on campus, especially following the Supreme Court’s ruling against affirmative action. Georgetown students formed Hoyas Against Legacy Admissions, a campaign that met with university administrators, sought faculty support, and successfully lobbied the DC State Board of Education to pass the FAIR Act (legislation that would ban legacy preference at DC universities) which is currently awaiting a vote in the DC City Council. The tension between the university’s institutional defense of the policy and growing student opposition raises a key question: how broadly is legacy admissions valued or accepted among the Georgetown student population?

This memo attempts to surface those students’ perspectives, with the goal of informing an admissions policy debate that has so far been dominated by administrators and outside advocates rather than the students most directly affected. It examines student attitudes toward legacy admissions using data from a random sample of Georgetown students administered in April 2026 (n = 385). We investigate what predicts opposition to legacy admissions and whether providing Georgetown-specific information about the program changes those attitudes. The survey included an embedded experiment, where half of respondents were randomly assigned to a control condition and asked a straightforward question about legacy admissions, while the other half received a treatment condition that first presented Georgetown’s own admit-rate and enrollment statistics before asking the same question.

1. Does Information Change Opinions? Treatment vs. Control

To test whether learning about Georgetown’s own legacy program moves opinion, we randomly assigned respondents to either a plain question (control) or the same question preceded by Georgetown-specific statistics (treatment).

Showing students Georgetown’s own legacy statistics didn’t move opinion much. Opposition was 55.7% in the control group and 61.1% in the treatment group, a modest difference. The one notable shift: “Strongly Oppose” held steady at about 20% in the control group and ticked up to 25% in the treatment group, suggesting the Georgetown framing may have intensified opposition among those already opposed rather than converting supporters. Attitudes on this issue appear largely fixed, with information alone not enough to meaningfully change them.

2. The Strongest Predictor: Ideology

Ideology is the strongest predictor of opposition to legacy admissions. Among conservatives, only 36.4% oppose legacy admissions, compared to 39.1% of moderates, 62.2% of liberals, and a striking 89.1% of very liberal respondents. This represents a more than 50 point increase in opposition from conservatives to very liberals, showing a clear and steep ideological gradient. Linear regression confirms this relationship: ideology has a coefficient of 0.22, meaning that for each step increase in liberal ideology, the probability of opposing legacy admissions increases by approximately 22 percentage points. This effect is highly statistically significant (p < 0.001). The magnitude of this difference suggests that legacy admissions are not viewed as a neutral policy — they are interpreted through ideological frameworks about fairness and inequality. For conservatives, lower opposition may reflect a greater acceptance of institutional traditions, while for very liberals, opposition is nearly universal, suggesting legacy admissions are seen as fundamentally illegitimate.

Moving in a more liberal direction is consistently and substantially associated with greater opposition to the policy. Ideology alone explains a meaningful share of the total variation in opposition — notable for a single predictor in survey data. Very liberal respondents at 89.1% opposition demonstrate that for the most ideologically committed, legacy admissions are seen as incompatible with core values of fairness and equal opportunity.

3. Opposition by Party ID

Party identification shows a similar but slightly weaker pattern compared to ideology. Among Democrats, 63.2% oppose legacy admissions, while independents show a nearly identical level at 60.4%. In contrast, only 16.7% of Republicans oppose legacy admissions, a roughly 46 point gap compared to Democrats. Respondents identifying as other show the highest opposition at 70.0%. The similarity between Democrats and independents suggests that opposition goes beyond strict partisan identity and reflects broader attitudes shared across non-Republican groups. The relatively small number of Republican respondents (n=12) means these findings should be interpreted cautiously, but the overall pattern reinforces the ideological divide observed earlier.

4. Opposition by Household Income

The relationship between income and opposition to legacy admissions is non-linear. Among the lowest-income respondents (under $50k), opposition is very high at 90%. Notably, opposition does not drop off cleanly from there, with middle income groups ($50k-$149k) showing opposition in the 71-82% range, while opposition dips most sharply among the $150k-$249k and $250k-$499k groups at 58.6% and 46.7% respectively, before rising again among the highest earners ($500k+) at 70.5%. The clearest pattern is that the upper-middle income bracket (i.e. the range most associated with Georgetown’s legacy admit population) shows the lowest opposition. This aligns with the expectation that those most likely to benefit from legacy admissions are less inclined to oppose it. The high opposition at both the lower end and very top of the income distribution suggests attitudes are not driven purely by economic self-interest, but by broader concerns about fairness and institutional legitimacy.

The most analytically interesting finding is the relative complacency of the upper-middle income group. Respondents in the $250,000-$499,000 range show the lowest opposition at 46.7%, and this is unlikely to be coincidental. Research on Georgetown’s legacy student population places the majority of legacy admits in precisely this income range. This means that respondents in this bracket are most likely to be the direct beneficiaries of legacy admissions or to know families who are. They are not the lower-income families most directly displaced by the practice, nor are they wealthy enough to feel insulated from admissions competition entirely. Their comparatively muted opposition reflects the policy’s most direct personal upside for this group. Legacy admissions is a conflict most acutely felt at the economic extremes, and it bears out in the data.

When income is observed alongside ideology, the relationship becomes more nuanced. Among conservatives and moderates, higher income is associated with lower opposition to legacy admissions. This finding illustrates that wealthier individuals in these groups are more likely to support or accept the policy. In contrast, liberals and very liberals maintain consistently high levels of opposition regardless of income level, with opposition remaining above 60% and approaching 90% among very liberals. This finding suggests that for liberals, ideological beliefs about fairness override any potential economic incentives to support legacy admissions. In other words, income matters more for conservatives, but less so for liberals. This interaction is important because it shows that attitudes are not determined by a single factor, but by the combination of economic status and political beliefs. Still, ideology is the dominant force when shaping opinions even when controlling for income.

5. Opposition by Gender

Not all variables examined proved to be meaningful predictors of opposition to legacy admissions. Gender and grade level showed no significant relationship with opposition and are not emphasized in this analysis. It is worth noting that women in the sample skew slightly more liberal than men. Given that ideology is by far the strongest predictor of opposition, any gender gap in opposition that does appear is more likely a downstream reflection of that ideological difference. Women are not more opposed to legacy admissions because of their gender but because ideology is doing the explanatory work, and women in this sample lean more liberal.

6. Opposition by Year in School

First-years are the most opposed (72%), with opposition softening slightly as students progress through school. Upperclassmen may become more accustomed to or aware of how institutional relationships work, though this difference is not statistically significant.

7. Does the Framing Work Differently by Ideology?

Among conservatives, opposition decreased from 50.0% in the control group to 28.6% in the treatment group, suggesting that exposure to Georgetown-specific legacy statistics may have reinforced existing support among conservatives rather than weakening it, though this finding should be interpreted cautiously given the small number of conservative respondents (n=11). Moderates moved in the opposite direction: opposition increased from 32.5% in the control group to 50.0% in the treatment group, suggesting the treatment had its strongest persuasive effect on respondents in the ideological middle.

8. Do Random and Self-Selected Respondents Differ?

Self-selected respondents (friends of students in the class) and randomly contacted students hold broadly similar views on legacy admissions, with 56.6% of self-selected respondents opposing legacy admissions compared to 61.0% of randomly contacted students. This difference is modest and not statistically significant, which is reassuring. A large gap here would suggest that people with strong opinions disproportionately sought out the survey, biasing the results. The absence of a significant difference increases confidence that our findings accurately reflect student opinion rather than selection effects.

Conclusion

This survey of Georgetown undergraduates reveals strong majority opposition to legacy admissions, with roughly 60% of respondents opposing the practice, a number that held stable regardless of whether they were shown Georgetown-specific statistics. This null treatment effect suggests that attitudes toward legacy admissions are well-formed and resistant to informational interventions, at least among this population of politically engaged college students.

The most consistent predictor of opposition is political ideology. At Georgetown, very liberal students oppose legacy at the highest rates (nearly 9 in 10), while conservatives show substantially lower opposition, reflecting how ideology shapes interpretations of fairness and institutional tradition. Household income, gender, and year in school show weaker and less consistent relationships once ideology is accounted for.
